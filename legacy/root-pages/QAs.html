Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about my research journey, PhD application plans, and academic aspirations

Research Motivation

My intellectual journey into computational psychology commenced during my undergraduate studies when I recognized the transformative potential of integrating rigorous psychological theories with sophisticated computational methodologies. While traditional psychological research often relies on small-scale studies and subjective interpretations, computational approaches enable researchers to analyze vast, complex datasets and uncover intricate behavioral patterns that would be impossible to detect through conventional manual analysis.

What particularly captivates me is how computational methodologies can effectively bridge the conceptual gap between individual-level psychological processes and large-scale social phenomena. For instance, in my current research on marital interactions, I employ advanced natural language processing techniques to systematically analyze thousands of spousal conversations, revealing sophisticated communication patterns that reliably predict relationship satisfaction outcomes. This unprecedented scale and precision of analysis was virtually unimaginable just a decade ago.

I firmly believe that computational psychology represents the future trajectory of psychological research, offering unprecedented opportunities to understand complex human behavior through sophisticated data-driven insights while maintaining the theoretical rigor and empirical standards that psychological science demands.

My transition was gradual but deliberate. It started during my undergraduate thesis when I encountered limitations in traditional statistical methods for analyzing complex behavioral data. I began self-learning Python and discovered the power of machine learning for psychological research.

The turning point came when I joined Dr. Xiaoyi Fang's lab at Beijing Normal University. Working on the NSFC-funded marital interaction project, I had the opportunity to apply computational methods to real psychological questions. I taught myself advanced NLP techniques, including BERT fine-tuning and topic modeling, specifically to address our research needs.

This hands-on experience taught me that computational skills aren't just tools—they're new ways of thinking about psychological phenomena. Each method I learned opened up new research questions and possibilities that I hadn't considered before.

Human behavior is inherently multimodal—we communicate through words, gestures, facial expressions, and physiological responses simultaneously. Traditional research often examines these modalities in isolation, missing the rich interactions between them.

My experience with fMRI data analysis and text processing has shown me how different data types can provide complementary insights. For example, while text analysis reveals what people say, neuroimaging shows how their brains process information, and behavioral measures capture their actual responses. Integrating these perspectives provides a more complete understanding of psychological processes.

I'm particularly excited about the potential of multimodal approaches for understanding complex phenomena like marital dynamics, where verbal communication, emotional expression, and physiological synchrony all play crucial roles. This holistic approach aligns with my belief that psychology should embrace the full complexity of human experience.

PhD Application

The United States offers unparalleled opportunities in computational psychology research. American universities have been pioneers in integrating sophisticated computational methodologies with psychological research, and many of the foundational techniques I employ—including advanced natural language processing frameworks and multimodal analysis approaches—were originally developed and refined in leading US research laboratories.

More importantly, the collaborative and interdisciplinary academic culture in American universities aligns perfectly with my research interests and methodological approach. Computational psychology requires sophisticated expertise from multiple fields—experimental psychology, computer science, advanced statistics, and cognitive neuroscience. US universities excel at fostering these essential cross-disciplinary collaborations and providing the institutional infrastructure necessary for cutting-edge interdisciplinary research.

I'm also drawn to the emphasis on methodological innovation and empirical rigor that characterizes American psychological research. The opportunity to collaborate with leading researchers who are actively pushing the boundaries of what's methodologically and theoretically possible in computational psychology—particularly in areas like large language model applications and multimodal neuroimaging—is incredibly appealing and aligns with my research trajectory.

Finally, the diversity of perspectives in American universities would enrich my research. Understanding human behavior requires insights from different cultural contexts, and the international research environment in the US would provide invaluable exposure to diverse approaches and viewpoints.

My primary research interest lies at the intersection of computational psychology and social relationships. I want to develop and apply advanced computational methods to understand how people form, maintain, and navigate complex social relationships.

Specifically, I'm interested in:

  • Relationship Dynamics: Using NLP and machine learning to analyze communication patterns in various relationship types (romantic, familial, professional) and predict relationship outcomes.
  • Multimodal Social Cognition: Integrating text, audio, video, and physiological data to understand how people process social information and make relationship decisions.
  • Computational Intervention Design: Developing data-driven approaches to design and evaluate psychological interventions for relationship improvement.
  • Cross-Cultural Relationship Patterns: Applying computational methods to understand how cultural factors influence relationship dynamics and communication styles.

These areas build directly on my current research experience while expanding into new methodological territories that would benefit from the advanced computational resources and collaborative environment available in US universities.

I bring a unique combination of psychological training, computational skills, and research experience that would enrich any program. My hands-on experience with large-scale data analysis and advanced NLP techniques would be valuable for collaborative projects and methodological development.

I'm particularly excited about mentoring opportunities. Having taught myself many computational skills, I understand the challenges students face when learning these methods. I would love to help other students bridge the gap between psychological theory and computational practice.

My international perspective would also contribute to the program's diversity. Having conducted research in China's unique cultural context, I can offer insights into cross-cultural psychological phenomena and help develop more globally applicable research methods.

Additionally, I'm committed to open science practices. I plan to share code, data (when ethically appropriate), and methodological innovations with the broader research community, contributing to the field's collective advancement.

Future Planning

My ultimate goal is to become a leading researcher in computational psychology, contributing to both theoretical understanding and practical applications. I envision establishing a research lab that specializes in multimodal analysis of social relationships and develops innovative computational methods for psychological research.

In the short term (next 5-7 years), I plan to:

  • Complete my PhD with high-impact publications in top-tier journals
  • Develop expertise in emerging areas like large language models for psychological research
  • Build collaborations with researchers across psychology, computer science, and neuroscience
  • Gain teaching experience and mentor undergraduate and graduate students

Long-term (10+ years), I aspire to:

  • Lead an independent research program at a top university
  • Secure major research funding (NSF, NIH) for innovative computational psychology projects
  • Develop computational tools and methods that become standard in the field
  • Train the next generation of computational psychologists
  • Bridge academic research with real-world applications in mental health and relationship counseling

I believe computational psychology is on the verge of a major transformation. Several trends will shape the field's future:

Large Language Models and Psychology: LLMs will revolutionize how we analyze and understand human language in psychological contexts. We'll move beyond simple sentiment analysis to sophisticated understanding of psychological states, personality traits, and relationship dynamics from text data.

Real-time Multimodal Analysis: Advances in sensor technology and edge computing will enable real-time analysis of behavior in natural settings. This will bridge the gap between laboratory studies and real-world behavior.

Personalized Psychological Interventions: Computational methods will enable highly personalized therapeutic interventions based on individual behavioral patterns and response histories.

Cross-Cultural Computational Psychology: As computational methods become more accessible globally, we'll see more diverse perspectives and cross-cultural validation of psychological theories.

I want to be at the forefront of these developments, contributing to both methodological innovations and their ethical implementation in psychological research and practice.

Cultural Adaptation

I've been actively preparing for this transition in multiple ways. Academically, I've been following American psychological research closely, reading papers from top US journals, and familiarizing myself with the research culture and methodological standards.

Language-wise, I've achieved a TOEFL score of 106, demonstrating my English proficiency. More importantly, I've been practicing academic English through writing research papers, presenting at conferences, and engaging with international researchers online.

Culturally, I've been learning about American academic culture through online courses, webinars, and conversations with current PhD students in the US. I understand the importance of proactive communication, collaborative research, and independent thinking in American graduate programs.

Practically, I've researched living arrangements, healthcare systems, and daily life logistics. I'm excited about the independence and responsibility that come with graduate study abroad.

My Chinese cultural background provides unique insights into psychological phenomena that are often overlooked in Western research. For example, concepts like "guanxi" (relationship networks) and collectivistic decision-making processes offer different perspectives on social psychology that could enrich American research.

I also bring experience with different research methodologies and data sources. Chinese social media platforms and communication patterns provide rich data for computational psychology research that could complement Western datasets and improve the generalizability of findings.

My experience navigating between individualistic and collectivistic cultural frameworks gives me a nuanced understanding of cultural differences in psychological processes. This perspective would be valuable for developing more culturally sensitive research methods and theories.

Additionally, my bilingual abilities would enable research on cross-linguistic psychological phenomena and help bridge Chinese and American research communities.

Academic Contribution

I aspire to make computational psychology more accessible and impactful for the broader psychological community. Many psychologists are intimidated by computational methods, creating a barrier to adopting these powerful tools. I want to develop user-friendly computational frameworks and educational resources that democratize access to advanced analytical methods.

Methodologically, I hope to contribute to the development of more robust and interpretable computational approaches for psychological research. Current methods often sacrifice interpretability for predictive power, but I believe we can develop approaches that excel at both.

Theoretically, I want to use computational methods to test and refine existing psychological theories while discovering new patterns that lead to novel theoretical insights. The scale and precision of computational analysis can reveal phenomena that traditional methods miss.

Practically, I hope my research will inform evidence-based interventions for improving relationships and mental health. The ultimate goal is to translate computational insights into tools that help people live better lives.

I believe strongly in translational research that bridges the gap between academic findings and practical applications. Throughout my PhD and beyond, I plan to maintain close connections with practitioners in clinical psychology, counseling, and relationship therapy.

I'm particularly interested in developing computational tools that can assist therapists and counselors in their work. For example, automated analysis of therapy session transcripts could help identify effective intervention strategies or predict treatment outcomes.

I also plan to engage with technology companies and app developers who are creating mental health and relationship tools. My research could inform the development of more effective, evidence-based digital interventions.

Additionally, I'm committed to science communication and public engagement. I want to make my research findings accessible to the general public through blogs, podcasts, and popular science writing, helping people understand and apply psychological insights in their daily lives.

Finally, I plan to collaborate with policymakers and organizations working on social issues related to relationships and mental health, ensuring that research insights inform evidence-based policies and programs.
